The Sun

G-type Main-Sequence Star

1.39Mkm Diameter
5,778°K Surface
99.86%System Mass
4.6BYears Old

The Sun is the star at the center of our solar system. It's a nearly perfect ball of hot plasma, generating energy through nuclear fusion of hydrogen into helium at its core. Its immense gravity holds the entire solar system together, from the innermost rocky planets to the farthest reaches of the Oort Cloud.

Mercury

Terrestrial Planet • Closest to the Sun

4,880km Diameter
88Days Orbit
430°CDay Temp
0Moons

Mercury is the smallest and innermost planet in the solar system. Despite being closest to the Sun, it is not the hottest planet — that title belongs to Venus. Mercury has a heavily cratered surface, similar to Earth's Moon, and experiences extreme temperature swings from 430°C during the day to -180°C at night.

Venus

Terrestrial Planet • Earth's "Sister"

12,104km Diameter
225Days Orbit
465°CSurface Temp
0Moons

Venus is the second planet from the Sun and the hottest in our solar system. Its thick atmosphere of carbon dioxide traps heat in a runaway greenhouse effect. Venus rotates backwards compared to most planets and a day on Venus (243 Earth days) is longer than its year (225 Earth days).

🌍

Earth

Terrestrial Planet • The Blue Marble

12,742km Diameter
365.25Days Orbit
15°CAverage Temp
1Moon

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only known celestial body to harbor life. About 71% of its surface is covered in water, giving it the distinctive blue appearance seen from space. Earth's atmosphere, magnetic field, and distance from the Sun create perfect conditions for liquid water and life.

Mars

Terrestrial Planet • The Red Planet

6,779km Diameter
687Days Orbit
-65°CAverage Temp
2Moons

Mars is known for its distinctive red color caused by iron oxide on its surface. It hosts Olympus Mons, the tallest volcano in the solar system at 21.9 km, and Valles Marineris, a 4,000 km canyon system. Scientists believe Mars once had liquid water billions of years ago.

Jupiter

Gas Giant • King of Planets

139,820km Diameter
11.86Years Orbit
-110°CCloud Temp
95Moons

Jupiter is the largest planet — over 1,300 Earths could fit inside. Its Great Red Spot is a storm raging for 400+ years, larger than Earth. Jupiter's powerful magnetic field and 95 known moons make it like a miniature solar system of its own.

🪐

Saturn

Gas Giant • The Ringed Planet

116,460km Diameter
29.46Years Orbit
-140°CCloud Temp
146Moons

Saturn is famous for its spectacular ring system made of billions of particles of ice and rock. Despite its size, Saturn is the least dense planet — it would float in water. Its moon Titan has a thick atmosphere and lakes of liquid methane.

Uranus

Ice Giant • The Tilted Planet

50,724km Diameter
84Years Orbit
-224°CCloud Temp
28Moons

Uranus rotates on its side with a 98-degree axial tilt, rolling around the Sun. Its blue-green color comes from methane absorbing red light. It was the first planet discovered by telescope, by William Herschel in 1781.
